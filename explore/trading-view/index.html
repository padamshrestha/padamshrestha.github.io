    Create trading view pinescript strategy to generate signals¬∂

    1. buy signal when the price is above chiku span and above ichimoku cloud
    2. sell signal when the price is below chiku span and below ichimoku cloud

    Here is an example of a TradingView PineScript strategy that generates buy and sell signals based on the conditions you specified:

    Python
    // Chikou Span
chikou = close[26]

// Ichimoku Cloud

kijun = (highest(high, 26) + lowest(low, 26)) / 2
senkou_a = (tenkan + kijun) / 2
senkou_b = (highest(high, 52) + lowest(low, 52)) / 2

// Buy and Sell Signals

    strategy.entry("Buy", strategy.long)

if close < chikou and close < senkou_a and close < senkou_b
    strategy.entry("Sell", strategy.short)

    Please note that this is a basic example and you can always customize it according to your specific requirements.

    How to calculate support and resistance in trading view prinscript from ichimoku strategy¬∂

    In TradingView PineScript, you can calculate support and resistance levels using the Ichimoku Kink≈ç Hy≈ç indicator by analyzing the positions of the Kijun-sen and Senkou Span B lines.

    The Kijun-sen line, also known as the base line, is a moving average of the highest high and lowest low over a period of 26 periods, and is commonly used as a support and resistance level. When the price is above the Kijun-sen line, it is considered to be in a bullish trend and the Kijun-sen line acts as a support level. When the price is below the Kijun-sen line, it is considered to be in a bearish trend and the Kijun-sen line acts as a resistance level.

    The Senkou Span B line, also known as the leading span B, is the average of the highest high and lowest low over a period of 52 periods, plotted 26 periods ahead. This line can also act as a support and resistance level. When the price is above the Senkou Span B line, it is considered to be in a bullish trend and the Senkou Span B line acts as a support level. When the price is below the Senkou Span B line, it is considered to be in a bearish trend and the Senkou Span B line acts as a resistance level.

    Here is an example of how you can calculate support and resistance levels using the Ichimoku Kink≈ç Hy≈ç indicator in TradingView PineScript:

    Python
    // Ichimoku Cloud
tenkan = (highest(high, 9) + lowest(low, 9)) / 2
kijun = (highest(high, 26) + lowest(low, 26)) / 2
senkou_a = (tenkan + kijun) / 2
senkou_b = (highest(high, 52) + lowest(low, 52)) / 2

// Support and Resistance Levels
if (close > kijun)
    plot(kijun, color = blue, linewidth = 2)
else
    plot(kijun, color = red, linewidth = 2)

if (close > senkou_b)
    plot(senkou_b, color = blue, linewidth = 2)
else
    plot(senkou_b, color = red, linewidth = 2)

    In this example, the Kijun-sen line is plotted in blue when the price is above it and in red when the price is below it, indicating a support level when it's blue and resistance level when it's red. Similarly, the Senkou Span B line is plotted in blue when the price is above it and in red when the price is below it, indicating a support level when it's blue and resistance level when it's red.

    It's important to note that support and resistance levels are not always exact and can be broken. Traders often use these levels as a guide for making trading decisions, and also it's important to have a good understanding of the market and to use multiple indicators and other market data to form a more complete picture of the market.

    What are the best strategies for day option trading¬∂

    Day trading options can be a high-risk, high-reward strategy that can be profitable for experienced traders. Here are a few strategies that day traders may use when trading options:

    1. Covered call: This strategy involves holding a long position in an underlying asset and selling call options against that position. The goal of this strategy is to generate income from the option premium while also limiting the potential loss on the underlying asset.
    2. Bullish vertical spread: This strategy involves buying a call option at a lower strike price and selling a call option at a higher strike price. The goal of this strategy is to profit from a bullish market move while also limiting the potential loss.
    3. Bearish vertical spread: This strategy involves buying a put option at a higher strike price and selling a put option at a lower strike price. The goal of this strategy is to profit from a bearish market move while also limiting the potential loss.
    4. Iron condor: This strategy involves selling a call option and a put option at different strike prices to generate income from the option premium. The goal of this strategy is to profit from a neutral market move while also limiting the potential loss.
    5. Straddle: This strategy involves buying both a call and a put option at the same strike price, with the goal of profiting from a large move in either direction in the underlying asset.

    It's important to keep in mind that options trading is a complex and high-risk strategy, and it's important to have a good understanding of the market and to use proper risk management techniques. Additionally, it's important to use multiple indicators and other market data to form a more complete picture of the market. It's also highly recommended to paper trade and backtest any strategy before using it in live trading.
